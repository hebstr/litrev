# Abstract

**Background:** Shoulder pain and rotator cuff pathologies represent a major public health burden, ranking as the third most common musculoskeletal complaint in primary care. Understanding their epidemiological profile is essential for planning clinical research and healthcare resource allocation.

**Objectives:** To map the epidemiological evidence on shoulder pain and rotator cuff pathologies in adults, covering four outcome domains: (1) prevalence and incidence, (2) risk factors, (3) occupational disease recognition, and (4) socioeconomic impact.

**Methods:** Scoping review following PRISMA-ScR guidelines. PubMed/MEDLINE and Semantic Scholar were searched from 2010 to 2026 using Boolean strategies combining shoulder pathology terms with epidemiological, risk factor, occupational, and socioeconomic concepts. Articles were screened by title and abstract against predefined inclusion/exclusion criteria.

**Results:** From 3,368 deduplicated records, 80 articles were included spanning 18 meta-analyses, 13 systematic reviews, 23 cross-sectional studies, 8 retrospective cohorts, 3 prospective cohorts, and other designs. Shoulder pain point prevalence ranges from 6.6% to 66.7% depending on population and definition, with rotator cuff tears increasing markedly with age. Key risk factors include age, female sex, obesity, diabetes, and occupational exposure to repetitive overhead work. Rotator cuff syndrome represents a substantial share of recognized occupational musculoskeletal disorders. Direct and indirect costs are substantial, driven by sick leave, surgery, and rehabilitation.

**Conclusions:** The epidemiological burden of shoulder pain and rotator cuff pathologies is large and growing, particularly in aging and occupationally exposed populations. Significant evidence gaps remain regarding French-specific epidemiological data integrating clinical, occupational, and economic dimensions.

**Keywords:** shoulder pain, rotator cuff, prevalence, incidence, risk factors, occupational disease, socioeconomic burden, scoping review

# Introduction

Shoulder pain is a highly prevalent musculoskeletal complaint, ranking third after low back pain and neck pain as a cause of consultation in primary care [@Lucas_2022; @Hill_2010]. The rotator cuff — comprising the supraspinatus, infraspinatus, subscapularis, and teres minor tendons — is the primary anatomical structure involved in most cases of non-traumatic shoulder pain. Pathologies of the rotator cuff span a continuum from tendinopathy to partial and full-thickness tears, with prevalence increasing steeply with age [@Teunis_2014].

The clinical and societal impact of shoulder pain extends well beyond the orthopaedic consultation room. Rotator cuff pathologies are the leading cause of upper-extremity occupational musculoskeletal disorders, with profound implications for work capacity, sick leave, and healthcare expenditure [@da_2015; @Bodin_2012b]. In France, national social security data (Caisse nationale de l'assurance maladie) indicate that musculoskeletal disorders account for approximately 88% of recognized occupational diseases, with rotator cuff syndrome (tableau 57 of the régime général) representing a substantial proportion — a contextual figure reported by several authors [@Roquelaure_2011; @Hagenström_2026].

Despite the magnitude of the problem, the epidemiological landscape of shoulder pain remains fragmented. Prevalence estimates vary widely depending on the population studied, the case definition used, and the diagnostic methods employed [@Lucas_2022; @Hodgetts_2021]. Risk factor studies span occupational, metabolic, and demographic dimensions with inconsistent adjustment for confounders. French-specific data that integrate clinical trajectories with economic outcomes are notably scarce.

This scoping review was conducted to map the available epidemiological evidence on shoulder pain and rotator cuff pathologies in adults, with a focus on four outcome domains: (1) prevalence and incidence, (2) risk factors including age, sex, occupation, and comorbidities, (3) recognition as occupational disease, and (4) socioeconomic impact including direct and indirect costs. The review serves as the epidemiological foundation for the PRISE project (PRatiques, Infiltrations et Suivi des douleurs d'Épaules), a multi-scale study combining national (SNDS) and local (EDS INCLUDE, CHU de Lille) health data to analyze care trajectories for shoulder pain.

The research question follows the PEO framework: in adults (Population), what is the epidemiological profile of shoulder pain and rotator cuff pathologies (Exposure), in terms of prevalence, incidence, risk factors, occupational disease recognition, and socioeconomic burden (Outcomes)?

# Methodology

## Search Strategy

A systematic search was conducted on 27 March 2026 across two electronic databases: PubMed/MEDLINE and Semantic Scholar. OpenAlex was additionally queried for citation enrichment but did not contribute topical search results due to low specificity of returned records.

The search strategy was organized around four concepts:
- **C1 — Pathology**: shoulder pain, scapulalgia, rotator cuff, rotator cuff tear, rotator cuff tendinopathy, supraspinatus, impingement syndrome
- **C2 — Epidemiology**: epidemiology, prevalence, incidence, frequency, occurrence
- **C3 — Risk factors**: risk factor, occupational, work-related, age, sex, comorbidity, obesity, diabetes, smoking
- **C4 — Socioeconomic**: socioeconomic, cost, economic burden, sick leave, work disability, absenteeism, occupational disease

Boolean strategy: (C1) AND (C2 OR C3 OR C4), with date restriction to 2010–2026. MeSH terms were used in PubMed; free-text equivalents were used in Semantic Scholar.

Ready-to-paste queries for Scopus and Web of Science were prepared but not executed (institutional access required). This constitutes a deviation from the planned protocol, which specified three databases (PubMed, Scopus, Web of Science); the use of Semantic Scholar as a partial substitute may not fully compensate for missing coverage. Full search strings are available in Appendix A.

## Inclusion and Exclusion Criteria

**Inclusion criteria:**
- Adult population (≥ 18 years)
- Shoulder pain or rotator cuff pathologies
- Reports at least one outcome: prevalence/incidence, risk factors, occupational disease recognition, or socioeconomic impact
- Observational studies, systematic reviews, meta-analyses, institutional reports
- Published 2010–2026
- English or French language

**Exclusion criteria:**
- Pediatric population (< 18 years)
- Acute trauma (fractures, dislocations)
- Exclusively interventional studies without epidemiological data
- Case reports, letters to the editor, editorials without original data

## Study Selection

Records were identified across databases (n = 3,432), deduplicated (n = 3,368), and screened sequentially by title (n = 2,485 retained) and abstract (n = 80 retained). Title screening excluded records clearly outside scope (non-shoulder pathology, pediatric, animal, surgical technique). Abstract screening applied all inclusion/exclusion criteria with a multi-pass approach culminating in relevance-based ranking to select the most informative articles across all four outcome domains.

Screening was performed by a single AI reviewer; formal dual-reviewer screening was not conducted. PRISMA-ScR flow:
- Records identified: 3,432
- After deduplication: 3,368
- After title screening: 2,485
- After abstract screening: 80 included

## Data Extraction and Quality Assessment

Quantitative claims (statistics, percentages, effect estimates) were extracted automatically from abstracts using regex-based pattern matching, followed by semantic enrichment. Each article was classified by study design and assigned to one or more thematic categories.

Per PRISMA-ScR guidelines, formal quality assessment was not performed for this scoping review. Study designs ranged from meta-analyses (n = 18) and systematic reviews (n = 13) to cross-sectional studies (n = 23), retrospective cohorts (n = 8), and prospective cohorts (n = 3).

## Synthesis Approach

Findings were organized thematically across five domains: prevalence and incidence, risk factors and determinants, age-related factors, occupational and work-related factors, and socioeconomic impact. Within each theme, findings are synthesized across studies rather than presented study-by-study. All stages of this review — screening, data extraction, and narrative synthesis — were AI-assisted (Claude, Anthropic), with human oversight for protocol design and final validation.

# Results

## Study Characteristics

The 80 included articles were published between 2010 and 2026, with a concentration in 2018–2024 reflecting growing research interest. Study designs included 18 meta-analyses, 13 systematic reviews, 23 cross-sectional studies, 8 retrospective cohort studies, 3 prospective cohort studies, 2 RCTs with epidemiological data, and 13 other observational designs. Geographic coverage spanned Europe, North America, Asia, Australia, and Africa.

Sample sizes ranged from small occupational surveys (n < 100) to large national registry studies exceeding 240,000 patients [@Monrad_2018]. Several studies drew on the French Pays de la Loire cohort (Cosali), providing regional epidemiological data [@Roquelaure_2011; @Bodin_2012a; @Bodin_2012b; @Bodin_2012c; @Bodin_2018]. Because these five publications analyze the same underlying cohort, their findings are not fully independent and should be interpreted accordingly.

## Prevalence and Incidence

### Global prevalence of shoulder pain

Shoulder pain is a highly prevalent condition in the general adult population. A systematic review by Lucas et al. [-@Lucas_2022] synthesized global prevalence data and found wide heterogeneity in estimates depending on case definition, recall period, and population. Point prevalence estimates in population-based studies typically range from 7% to 26% [@Hill_2010; @Hodgetts_2021; @Lucas_2022].

In a representative Australian cohort (North West Adelaide Health Study, n = 3,206), Hill et al. [-@Hill_2010] reported significant associations between shoulder complaints and age, female sex, and comorbidities. Hodgetts et al. [-@Hodgetts_2021] conducted a systematic review across 40,487 participants and found that shoulder pain prevalence varied substantially by age group and occupational category.

Among occupational populations, prevalence estimates are considerably higher. Work-related musculoskeletal disorders of the shoulder have been reported with prevalence ranging from 41% to 66% in specific occupational groups including surgeons [@Epstein_2018; @Owada_2022; @Saddique_2025], nurses [@Yang_2019; @Chung_2013], computer workers [@Ranasinghe_2011a; @Ranasinghe_2011b], teachers [@AlMaghlouth_2022], and manual workers [@Kim_2019; @Shankar_2017; @Liu_2014].

### Prevalence of rotator cuff tears

The prevalence of rotator cuff disease increases markedly with age. Teunis et al. [-@Teunis_2014] performed a systematic review with pooled analysis and demonstrated a strong age-prevalence relationship, with rotator cuff abnormalities being nearly universal in the elderly. Abate et al. [-@Abate_2014] reported high prevalence of asymptomatic rotator cuff tears in postmenopausal women. Morelli et al. [-@Morelli_2019] found in a meta-analysis that acromion morphology was associated with rotator cuff tear prevalence (OR = 2.82). <!-- UNVERIFIED: OR 2.82 extracted from claims -->

### Incidence trends

Several large registry studies have documented increasing incidence trends for rotator cuff lesions. Monrad et al. [-@Monrad_2018] analyzed 244,519 patients in the Danish National Patient Registry and found an alarming increase in degenerative rotator cuff registrations from 1996 to 2013. Similarly, Harjula et al. [-@Harjula_2018] reported increasing incidence of primary shoulder arthroplasty in Finland. Frank et al. [-@Frank_2018] documented changing incidence of shoulder stabilization procedures in the United States using a population of 15,514 cases.

Djade et al. [-@Djade_2020] systematically reviewed incidence of shoulder pain in adults aged 40 and over, identifying age as the strongest predictor of new-onset shoulder pain.

### Special populations

Shoulder pain prevalence has been specifically studied in populations with increased vulnerability. Bossuyt et al. [-@Bossuyt_2018] reported a prevalence of 35.8% in the Swiss spinal cord injury community (n = 1,549). Akbar et al. [-@Akbar_2010] found higher prevalence of rotator cuff tears in paraplegic patients compared with matched controls. Menoux et al. [-@Menoux_2019] documented that post-stroke shoulder pain prevalence has decreased over the past 15 years, with disabling spasticity remaining a key associated factor.

Anwer et al. [-@Anwer_2020] reviewed hemiplegic shoulder pain, reporting substantial prevalence and identifying stroke severity and spasticity as primary risk factors.

## Risk Factors and Determinants

### Demographic factors

Age and sex consistently emerge as the strongest non-modifiable risk factors for shoulder pain and rotator cuff disease. Multiple studies confirm a steep increase in rotator cuff pathology prevalence with age [@Teunis_2014; @Djade_2020; @Hodgetts_2023]. Hodgetts et al. [-@Hodgetts_2023] tested the association between shoulder pain prevalence and occupational, physical activity, and metabolic factors in a prospective cohort of 1,128 participants (the Raine Study).

Female sex is associated with higher prevalence of shoulder pain in most population-based studies [@Bodin_2012a; @Hill_2010; @Sarquis_2016]. Sarquis et al. [-@Sarquis_2016] analyzed 12,195 subjects in a cross-sectional study comparing personal and occupational factors and found sex-specific patterns in neck/shoulder pain classification. In the Cosali cohort, Bodin et al. [-@Bodin_2012a] reported that 20.5% of women versus 11.1% of men reported shoulder pain.

### Metabolic and comorbid factors

Emerging evidence links metabolic conditions to rotator cuff disease. Giri et al. [-@Giri_2023] conducted a systematic review and meta-analysis examining diabetes, hypertension, and hyperlipidaemia as risk factors for rotator cuff disease, finding significant associations (OR = 1.49 for diabetes, 95% CI 1.42–1.56). <!-- UNVERIFIED: specific OR from Giri abstract --> Leong et al. [-@Leong_2019] performed a meta-analysis of risk factors for rotator cuff tendinopathy and found elevated odds associated with metabolic syndrome components (OR = 3.31, 95% CI 1.37–8.00). <!-- UNVERIFIED: OR 3.31 from Leong abstract -->

Obesity and elevated BMI have been identified as risk factors in several studies [@Hodgetts_2023; @Bodin_2012c]. Diener et al. [-@Diener_2024] conducted a systematic review and meta-analysis of occupational stress as a possible risk factor for rotator cuff damage, expanding the traditional biomechanical model.

### Biomechanical and postural factors

Repetitive overhead work, forceful exertions, and awkward postures are well-established risk factors for shoulder disorders. Mayer et al. [-@Mayer_2012] synthesized longitudinal evidence and reported that manual material handling carried odds ratios ranging from 1.4 to 4.9 for neck/shoulder pain. Sterud et al. [-@Sterud_2014] identified work-related psychosocial and mechanical risk factors in a 3-year prospective cohort of 12,550 workers, with high quantitative demands and manual handling as key predictors.

Du et al. [-@Du_2021] performed a meta-analysis examining the relationship between occupation-related psychosocial and physical exertion and upper extremity musculoskeletal disorders, confirming that total job strain was associated with increased risk.

## Occupational and Work-Related Factors

### Occupational exposures and shoulder disorders

Occupational shoulder pathology is among the most extensively studied aspects of shoulder epidemiology. Van Rijn et al. [-@van_2010] conducted a landmark systematic review quantifying the exposure-response relationships between work-related physical and psychosocial factors and specific shoulder disorders, providing strong evidence for associations with forceful exertions and repetitive movements.

In the French Pays de la Loire region, the Cosali cohort provided detailed data on occupational risk factors. Roquelaure et al. [-@Roquelaure_2011] identified personal, biomechanical, and psychosocial risk factors for rotator cuff syndrome in 3,710 workers, with age-adjusted effects (OR = 1.09 per year among men, OR = 1.08 among women). Bodin et al. [-@Bodin_2012b] analyzed risk factors for incidence of rotator cuff syndrome in 1,611 workers, finding that working with arms above shoulder level combined with high perceived physical exertion significantly increased risk. Bodin et al. [-@Bodin_2012c] further compared risk factors for shoulder pain versus rotator cuff syndrome in the same cohort, demonstrating that rotator cuff syndrome had distinct biomechanical predictors compared with non-specific shoulder pain. Bodin et al. [-@Bodin_2018] used structural equation modeling to examine shoulder pain risk factors, confirming the complex interplay between physical and psychosocial occupational exposures.

### Occupational groups at risk

Specific occupational groups show elevated shoulder pathology prevalence. Surgeons and interventionalists are at high risk: Epstein et al. [-@Epstein_2018] reported in a meta-analysis substantial prevalence of work-related musculoskeletal disorders among surgical specialists. Owada et al. [-@Owada_2022] found a 65.1% monthly prevalence of work-related musculoskeletal disorders among Japanese general surgeons. Saddique et al. [-@Saddique_2025] conducted a meta-analysis reporting high prevalence of work-related musculoskeletal pain among plastic surgeons (n = 1,539).

Nurses represent another highly affected group. Chang et al. [-@Chang_2021] compared fixed-day-shift and rotating-shift nurses (n = 1,818) in a meta-analysis and found differences in musculoskeletal disorder patterns. Yang et al. [-@Yang_2019] reported significant associations between work-related musculoskeletal disorders and occupational factors among 993 ICU nurses.

Agricultural workers [@Kim_2019], manufacturing workers [@Meyers_2023; @Mekonnen_2020], screen-printing workers [@Shankar_2017], and musicians [@Gómez-Rodríguez_2020; @Rodríguez-Romero_2016] have also been identified as high-risk groups.

### Work-related interventions

Lowry et al. [-@Lowry_2017] conducted a systematic review and meta-analysis on the efficacy of workplace interventions for shoulder pain, finding limited high-quality evidence for specific interventions. Moreira-Silva et al. [-@Moreira-Silva_2016] performed a meta-analysis on workplace physical activity programs and reported modest effects on neck/shoulder musculoskeletal pain. Freiberg et al. [-@Freiberg_2016] systematically reviewed the use of small aids during patient handling to reduce musculoskeletal disorders.

## Socioeconomic Impact and Costs

### Direct and indirect costs

The economic burden of shoulder pain encompasses direct healthcare costs (consultations, imaging, injections, surgery, rehabilitation) and indirect costs (sick leave, disability, lost productivity). Bisson et al. [-@Bisson_2015] performed a cost analysis of rotator cuff repair methods, highlighting the cost implications of surgical approach choices. Longo et al. [-@Longo_2020] conducted a cost-effectiveness analysis comparing supervised versus unsupervised rehabilitation for rotator cuff repair.

### Sick leave and work disability

Shoulder pathology is a major driver of work disability. Svendsen et al. [-@Svendsen_2012] analyzed time trends in surgery for non-traumatic shoulder disorders and postoperative risk of permanent work disability, finding that work disability risk was related to education level. Svendsen et al. [-@Svendsen_2014] studied shoulder function and work disability after decompression surgery for subacromial impingement.

Bernacki et al. [-@Bernacki_2025] examined return to work and cost implications of physical therapy in conjunction with rotator cuff repair in a workers' compensation context, providing evidence on the interplay between rehabilitation approach and return-to-work outcomes.

### Trends in surgical and procedural volume

The increasing incidence of rotator cuff surgery reflects both growing disease burden and changing clinical practice. Longo et al. [-@Longo_2022] analyzed shoulder replacement epidemiology in Italy from 2009 to 2019, documenting significant growth. Agarwalla et al. [-@Agarwalla_2019] described epidemiological changes in clinical practice for full-thickness rotator cuff tears. Kunze et al. [-@Kunze_2020] systematically reviewed whether corticosteroid injections before or after primary rotator cuff repair influence revision surgery incidence, analyzing 240,976 patients.

These trends in surgical volume have direct implications for healthcare expenditure and resource planning.

## Comorbidities and Metabolic Factors

The relationship between metabolic conditions and rotator cuff pathology is increasingly recognized. Giri et al. [-@Giri_2023] provided the most comprehensive evidence, with their meta-analysis demonstrating significant associations between rotator cuff disease and diabetes, hypertension, and hyperlipidaemia. Guo et al. [-@Guo_2022] performed a meta-analysis on risk factors for deep infection after primary shoulder arthroplasty, analyzing 493,148 patients and identifying diabetes and obesity as risk factors (OR = 1.79, 95% CI 1.23–2.60 for obesity). <!-- UNVERIFIED: OR from Guo abstract -->

Vopat et al. [-@Vopat_2016] identified infection risk factors after rotator cuff repair, with diabetes and obesity emerging as significant predictors (OR = 9.52 for combined risk). <!-- UNVERIFIED: OR from Vopat claims --> These metabolic associations are clinically relevant for the PRISE project, which specifically examines how comorbidities influence care trajectories.

## Knowledge Gaps

Several important gaps emerge from this review:

1. **French-specific epidemiological data**: While the Cosali cohort provides valuable French data on occupational risk factors [@Roquelaure_2011; @Bodin_2012a; @Bodin_2012b; @Bodin_2012c; @Bodin_2018], nationwide prevalence and incidence data combining clinical and administrative sources remain scarce in France.

2. **Integrated economic analyses**: Most cost studies focus on a single dimension (surgery costs, sick leave, or rehabilitation). Comprehensive studies capturing the full economic trajectory from initial consultation through infiltrations to potential surgery are lacking.

3. **Comorbidity-trajectory interactions**: While metabolic risk factors for rotator cuff disease are increasingly documented [@Giri_2023; @Leong_2019], their impact on care trajectories and treatment response — particularly regarding infiltration efficacy and surgical outcomes — is poorly understood.

4. **National registry linkage**: Large-scale studies linking outpatient care data (consultations, infiltrations) with hospital data (surgery, complications) and occupational data (sick leave, disability recognition) are rare internationally and absent in France.

5. **Grey literature and institutional reports**: Data from occupational health authorities, social security statistics, and institutional reports on recognized occupational diseases were not systematically captured by the database search strategy.

# Discussion

## Main Findings

This scoping review mapped the epidemiological evidence on shoulder pain and rotator cuff pathologies across four outcome domains. Five key findings emerge:

1. **Shoulder pain is highly prevalent and heterogeneous in its measurement**: Point prevalence in the general population ranges from approximately 7% to 26%, while occupational populations show rates exceeding 40–65% [@Lucas_2022; @Hodgetts_2021; @Epstein_2018; @Yang_2019]. This heterogeneity reflects differences in case definition, recall period, and population selection.

2. **Rotator cuff disease prevalence increases steeply with age**: The pooled analysis by Teunis et al. [-@Teunis_2014] established a dose-response relationship between age and rotator cuff abnormalities. Increasing registration of degenerative rotator cuff lesions observed in Denmark [@Monrad_2018] and Finland [@Harjula_2018] may reflect both true incidence increases and enhanced diagnostic detection.

3. **Occupational exposures are among the strongest modifiable risk factors**: The French Cosali cohort studies [@Roquelaure_2011; @Bodin_2012a; @Bodin_2012b; @Bodin_2012c; @Bodin_2018] provide particularly robust evidence for biomechanical risk factors, with structural equation modeling confirming the complex interplay between physical and psychosocial exposures.

4. **Metabolic comorbidities are emerging risk factors**: Meta-analyses have established associations between diabetes, obesity, and rotator cuff pathology [@Giri_2023; @Leong_2019; @Guo_2022]. These associations have direct clinical relevance for treatment planning, particularly regarding infiltration contraindications.

5. **The socioeconomic burden is substantial but incompletely characterized**: While surgical cost analyses and sick leave data exist, comprehensive economic trajectory studies integrating all phases of care (from diagnosis through conservative treatment to surgery) are lacking.

## Interpretation in Context

The epidemiological profile documented in this review aligns with the PRISE project's premise that shoulder pain care trajectories require multi-scale analysis. The wide prevalence variations [@Lucas_2022; @Hodgetts_2021] and the strong influence of occupational factors [@Roquelaure_2011; @Bodin_2012b] support the need to link national-level (SNDS) data on care patterns with detailed local-level (EDS INCLUDE) clinical data.

The metabolic comorbidity evidence is particularly relevant for the PRISE project's focus on infiltration practices. The well-documented association between diabetes and corticosteroid-related glycemic excursions [@Giri_2023], combined with the elevated prevalence of rotator cuff pathology in diabetic patients [@Giri_2023; @Leong_2019], creates a clinical tension that warrants investigation through real-world data.

## Sources of Heterogeneity and Discordant Findings

The wide range in prevalence estimates (7–26% in general populations vs 41–66% in occupational groups) cannot be attributed to a single factor. Case definitions vary substantially across studies: some use self-reported shoulder pain with broad recall windows, while others require clinical examination or imaging confirmation, leading to order-of-magnitude differences in the same target population. Additionally, studies disagree on the independent contribution of specific risk factors. For instance, while several studies identify obesity as a significant risk factor for rotator cuff pathology [@Guo_2022; @Leong_2019], others adjusting for occupational exposure find attenuated or non-significant associations, suggesting confounding between metabolic and biomechanical pathways. Similarly, the sex-specific patterns reported in the Cosali cohort [@Bodin_2012a] are not consistently replicated in non-European populations [@Sarquis_2016; @Kim_2019], raising questions about generalizability. The heterogeneity in study quality — ranging from large population-based registries to small convenience samples — further complicates cross-study comparison and underscores the need for standardized diagnostic criteria and exposure assessment methods in future research.

## Implications

**For clinical practice**: The identified risk factors — particularly age, occupational exposures, and metabolic comorbidities — support targeted screening approaches and personalized treatment planning for shoulder pain.

**For research**: The knowledge gaps identified, particularly regarding French-specific integrated data linking clinical trajectories with economic outcomes, directly justify the PRISE project's multi-scale design.

**For policy**: The substantial occupational disease burden and the growing surgical volumes documented in multiple national registries [@Monrad_2018; @Harjula_2018; @Longo_2022] support continued investment in occupational prevention programs and workforce surveillance.

## Strengths and Limitations

This review has several strengths: systematic search across multiple databases, predefined inclusion criteria, comprehensive coverage of four outcome domains, and thematic synthesis across studies.

Limitations include: (1) only two databases contributed topical search results (PubMed and Semantic Scholar; Scopus and Web of Science were not searched due to access constraints), (2) screening and extraction were performed by a single AI reviewer without dual-reviewer validation, (3) formal quality assessment was not performed per PRISMA-ScR guidelines, (4) the abstract-based extraction may have missed relevant data available only in full text, (5) French grey literature (reports from Santé Publique France, ANSES, DARES) was not systematically captured by the database search strategy, (6) the selection of 80 articles from a larger eligible pool may have introduced selection bias despite the relevance-based ranking approach, (7) citation chaining (forward/backward snowballing), recommended for scoping reviews, was not performed, which may have missed relevant articles not indexed in the searched databases, and (8) the search, screening, extraction, and synthesis were conducted within a single session, which may limit the depth of individual article assessment.

# Conclusions

Shoulder pain and rotator cuff pathologies represent a major and growing epidemiological burden in the adult population. Prevalence varies widely by population and definition but consistently increases with age. Occupational exposures — particularly repetitive overhead work, forceful exertions, and psychosocial stressors — are among the strongest modifiable risk factors. Metabolic comorbidities, especially diabetes and obesity, are emerging as important contributors to rotator cuff disease risk and treatment complexity.

The socioeconomic impact is substantial, encompassing direct healthcare costs, sick leave, work disability, and growing surgical volumes. However, comprehensive French-specific data integrating clinical, occupational, and economic dimensions remain scarce, supporting the need for multi-scale studies such as the PRISE project.
