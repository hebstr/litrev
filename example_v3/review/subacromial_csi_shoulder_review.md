# Abstract {-}

**Background**: Subacromial corticosteroid injections (CSI) are among the most commonly used conservative treatments for shoulder pain and rotator cuff pathology, yet their efficacy, optimal technique, and impact on downstream surgical outcomes remain debated.
**Objectives**: To map the current evidence on subacromial corticosteroid injections in adults with shoulder pain regarding (1) efficacy on pain, function, and satisfaction; (2) predictive factors of response or failure; (3) place in the care sequence relative to physiotherapy and surgery; and (4) complications and tendon effects.
**Methods**: A scoping review following PRISMA-ScR guidelines was conducted. PubMed/MEDLINE, Semantic Scholar, and OpenAlex were searched for studies published between 2010 and 2026. Inclusion criteria: adults (>=18 years), subacromial or periarticular corticosteroid injections for shoulder pain or rotator cuff pathology, RCTs, cohort studies, systematic reviews, meta-analyses, clinical guidelines, and observational studies in English or French.
**Results**: From 552 identified records, 358 articles were included after title, abstract, and full-text screening. Seven thematic areas were identified: CSI efficacy and comparative injection therapies (251 articles), CSI versus physiotherapy and exercise (131), injection technique and image guidance (117), safety and adverse effects (101), CSI for adhesive capsulitis and frozen shoulder (67), CSI impact on rotator cuff repair and surgical outcomes (64), and calcific tendinopathy management (41). The evidence consistently shows short-term pain relief from CSI (up to 8 weeks), but limited long-term benefit beyond 3-6 months. Ultrasound guidance improves accuracy for certain injection sites. Preoperative CSI within 6 months of rotator cuff repair may increase revision risk. Alternative injectables (PRP) show comparable or superior long-term outcomes.
**Conclusions**: CSI provides reliable short-term analgesia for shoulder pain and rotator cuff disorders but should be viewed as a bridge therapy rather than a definitive treatment. Combined with physiotherapy, CSI appears most effective as a first-line approach. Timing relative to surgery and cumulative tendon effects warrant careful clinical consideration.
**Keywords**: corticosteroid injection, shoulder pain, rotator cuff, subacromial, scoping review, physiotherapy, adhesive capsulitis, injection guidance

# Introduction

## Background and Context

Shoulder pain is one of the most prevalent musculoskeletal complaints, affecting up to 30% of primary care consultations for musculoskeletal disorders [@Azevedo_2020]. Subacromial impingement syndrome (SIS) encompasses a spectrum of pathology ranging from subacromial bursitis to rotator cuff tendinopathy and full-thickness tears [@Harrison_2011]. Within the conservative management armamentarium, corticosteroid injections (CSI) have been a mainstay for decades, used to reduce inflammation, alleviate pain, and facilitate rehabilitation.

The PRISE protocol at CHU de Lille incorporates CSI as a component of a structured care pathway for shoulder pain. Understanding the current state of evidence regarding the injection component is essential for optimizing patient outcomes and refining institutional practice.

## Scope and Objectives

This scoping review maps the available evidence on subacromial corticosteroid injections in the management of shoulder pain in adults, structured around four domains derived from the PICO framework:

**Primary Research Questions:**

1. What is the efficacy of subacromial CSI on pain (VAS/NRS), function scores, and patient satisfaction, compared with placebo, conservative treatments, or other injectables?
2. What are the predictive factors of response or failure following CSI?
3. Where does CSI sit in the care sequence relative to physiotherapy, other conservative measures, and surgery?
4. What are the complications, adverse effects, and tendon-level impacts of repeated corticosteroid exposure?

**Framework**: PICO

- **Population**: Adults (>=18 years) with shoulder pain or rotator cuff pathology
- **Intervention**: Subacromial corticosteroid injections (ultrasound-guided or landmark-guided)
- **Comparator**: Conservative treatment (physiotherapy, analgesics), placebo, or direct surgery
- **Outcomes**: Efficacy (pain, function, satisfaction); predictive factors; place in care sequence; complications and tendon effects

## Significance

Despite widespread clinical use, the optimal role of CSI within structured care pathways remains uncertain. Recent large RCTs have questioned long-term benefits [@Hopewell_2021a; @Beard_2018], while emerging evidence on biologics such as platelet-rich plasma (PRP) has introduced new comparators [@Rossi_2024; @Kwong_2021]. This scoping review synthesizes a large and heterogeneous evidence base to identify consensus areas, persistent controversies, and knowledge gaps that can inform both clinical practice and future research priorities.

# Methodology

## Protocol

This scoping review follows the PRISMA-ScR (Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses extension for Scoping Reviews) guideline. The protocol was defined a priori with a PICO framework.

**Deviations**: None.

**Search Date:** 2026-04-02

## Inclusion and Exclusion Criteria

**Inclusion Criteria:**

- Adults >=18 years
- Subacromial or periarticular corticosteroid injections for shoulder pain / rotator cuff pathology
- Study designs: RCTs, cohort studies, case-control, cross-sectional, systematic reviews, meta-analyses, clinical guidelines, observational studies
- Published 2010-2026
- Language: English or French

**Exclusion Criteria:**

- Pediatric populations (<18 years)
- Tumor pathologies
- Non-corticosteroid injections (PRP, hyaluronic acid, stem cells) as primary intervention (acceptable as comparator)
- Case reports, editorials, conference abstracts only
- Animal studies

**Exclusion reasons at abstract screening (n=64):** No mention of corticosteroid injection (n=52), non-CSI primary intervention without CSI comparator (n=5), animal study (n=2), not about shoulder pathology (n=5).

**Exclusion reasons at full-text screening (n=3):** Commentary on Cochrane review (n=1), conference abstract (n=1), no mention of CSI (n=1).

## Data Extraction

**Method:** Automated extraction from abstracts using regex-based quantitative claim extraction and LLM-enriched semantic claim extraction.
**Items:** Study metadata (title, authors, year, DOI, PMID), quantitative claims (statistics, percentages, p-values, confidence intervals, sample sizes), semantic claims (efficacy direction, claim type, population, effect size), and theme assignment.

**Extraction summary:** 4,195 quantitative claims and 981 semantic claims were extracted across 348 articles with available abstracts.

## Quality Assessment

Per PRISMA-ScR, formal quality assessment is not required for scoping reviews. Quality indicators (study design, sample size, bias risk) were noted during extraction but no formal risk-of-bias tool was applied systematically.

## Synthesis and Analysis

**Approach:** Narrative thematic synthesis. Articles were assigned to one or more of seven themes identified through LLM-assisted topic analysis. Findings are synthesized across studies within each theme, with cross-theme integration in the Discussion.

# Results

## Study Selection

**Summary:** 552 records were identified across three databases (PubMed: 304, Semantic Scholar: 175, OpenAlex: 73). After title screening (425 retained), abstract screening (348 retained), and full-text assessment of no-abstract articles (358 retained), 358 articles were included.

**Publication years:** The included studies span 2010-2026, with increasing publication volume over time. Peak years were 2023 (n=35), 2024 (n=31), 2019 (n=28), and 2021 (n=28), reflecting growing research interest in the topic.

**Study types:** The evidence base includes RCTs, systematic reviews with and without meta-analysis, cohort studies, cross-sectional studies, clinical guidelines, network meta-analyses, and qualitative studies. A substantial proportion of articles are systematic reviews or meta-analyses, providing synthesized evidence alongside primary studies.

**Geography:** Studies originate from diverse settings including North America, Europe (with French data prioritized), Asia, and the Middle East.

**Sources by database:** PubMed-search (304), S2-search (175), OpenAlex-search (73).

## Study Characteristics

The 358 included articles encompass heterogeneous study designs and populations. Given the scoping nature of this review and the large article count, a comprehensive study characteristics table is provided in Appendix D. Key characteristics are summarized below:

- **Population ages:** Most studies included adults aged 18-80 years, with mean ages typically ranging from 40 to 65 years
- **Conditions studied:** Subacromial impingement syndrome, rotator cuff tendinopathy, adhesive capsulitis/frozen shoulder, calcific tendinopathy, rotator cuff tears (partial and full-thickness)
- **Interventions:** Subacromial CSI (most common), glenohumeral intra-articular CSI, hydrodilatation with corticosteroid, periarticular CSI post-surgery
- **Corticosteroids used:** Triamcinolone acetonide (most frequent, typically 40 mg), methylprednisolone, betamethasone, dexamethasone
- **Comparators:** Placebo/saline, physiotherapy/exercise, PRP, hyaluronic acid, NSAID injections, shockwave therapy, surgery

## Thematic Synthesis

### CSI Efficacy and Comparative Injection Therapies

This theme, the largest in the review (251 articles), addresses the core question of CSI effectiveness for shoulder pain and function, including comparisons with alternative injectables.

**Short-term efficacy.** There is broad consensus that subacromial CSI provides significant short-term pain relief and functional improvement, typically measurable within 2-8 weeks of injection. The GRASP trial, a large multicentre RCT (n=708), found that subacromial CSI resulted in improvement in shoulder pain and function at 8 weeks compared with no injection (adjusted mean difference -5.64, 99% CI -9.93 to -1.34) [@Hopewell_2021a; @Hopewell_2021b]. Corticosteroid injections provide significant symptom relief for 2 to 24 weeks in adhesive capsulitis [@Xiao_2017]. Multiple systematic reviews confirm this short-term benefit across shoulder pathologies [@Coombes_2010; @Wang_2017; @Dong_2015].

**Long-term outcomes.** However, the long-term benefit of CSI is consistently questioned. The GRASP trial found no evidence of a difference between CSI and no injection over 12 months (adjusted mean difference -1.11, 99% CI -4.47 to 2.26) [@Hopewell_2021a]. This pattern of short-term benefit without sustained improvement is echoed across meta-analyses [@Coombes_2010; @Wang_2017]. Intra-articular corticosteroid injections were more effective in pain relief in the short term, but this pain relief did not sustain in the long term [@Wang_2017].

**CSI versus NSAID injections.** Nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory injections (SNIs) appear as effective as corticosteroid injections for short-term pain relief and improving function in subacromial impingement syndrome, while being less expensive and causing no major difference in complications [@Ziradkar_2023]. Ketorolac infiltration has shown equivalent outcomes to steroid infiltration for subacromial impingement [@Goyal_2022].

**CSI versus PRP.** An evolving body of evidence compares CSI with platelet-rich plasma (PRP). Corticosteroids show better short-term efficacy, whereas PRP demonstrates advantages in long-term functional recovery [@Peng_2023; @Sun_2022]. A double-blind RCT found that at 12 months, patients in the PRP group showed significantly greater improvement in VAS scores than the CSI group, and the overall failure rate was significantly higher in the corticosteroid group [@Rossi_2024]. A meta-analysis of 13 RCTs (n=725) confirmed that PRP was not worse than CSI at any time point and may provide better medium-to-long-term improvements [@Pang_2023]. However, PRP provided statistically worse short-term changes in ASES scores and SST scores compared with CSI [@Pang_2023].

**CSI alone versus combined approaches.** CSI alone demonstrated inferior outcomes across pain, patient-reported outcome measures, and range of motion (ROM) compared with combined approaches. Physical therapy combined with subacromial CSI is recommended as a first-line treatment, followed by acromioplasty and physiotherapy if conservative management fails [@Lavoie-Gagne_2022]. Physiotherapy plus CSI showed more long-lasting effects than physiotherapy alone on pain intensity, disability, and quality of life at medium-term follow-up [@Raeesi_2023].

**Hydrodilatation.** Hydrodilatation with corticosteroids has been identified as the most effective conservative management for frozen shoulder, providing advantages over placebo in short-term pain relief, ROM, and function [@Lädermann_2021].

### CSI Versus Physiotherapy and Exercise

This theme (131 articles) examines the comparative and combined effectiveness of CSI and physiotherapy-based interventions.

**Head-to-head comparisons.** The GRASP trial demonstrated that progressive exercise was not superior to a best practice advice session with a physiotherapist, and subacromial CSI provided only modest short-term benefit [@Hopewell_2021a; @Hopewell_2021b]. A Cochrane review of 32 trials (1,836 participants) found that manual therapy and exercise may not be as effective as glucocorticoid injection in the short term for adhesive capsulitis, with a mean difference in pain of 26 points on a 100-point scale favouring injection at seven weeks [@Page_2014]. However, group differences in improvement at 6 and 12 months were not clinically important [@Page_2014].

**Exercise therapy in frozen shoulder.** For frozen shoulder specifically, while both exercise and oral corticosteroids achieved significant improvements, the exercise group was superior in terms of functional outcomes, with larger effect sizes [@Çelik_2023]. This suggests that CSI should be seen as a facilitator of early rehabilitation rather than a substitute for exercise.

**Combined therapy.** The combination of CSI with physiotherapy appears to be the optimal first-line approach. When significant symptoms persist, physical therapy combined with CSI is recommended before escalating to surgical intervention [@Lavoie-Gagne_2022; @Hohmann_2022]. Expert consensus confirms that the first line of treatment should always be physiotherapy, and a corticosteroid injection can be helpful in reducing symptoms to enable engagement with rehabilitation [@Hohmann_2022].

**Clinical decision-making.** Qualitative research exploring physiotherapist decision-making around CSI for subacromial pain identified three broad factors: initial management approach, patient factors, and therapist beliefs, with seven sub-themes influencing referral patterns [@Lee_2020]. Knowledge assessment of family physicians and physiotherapists showed that more than 80% of physiotherapists and 84% of family physicians correctly diagnosed shoulder conditions, though practices diverging from evidence-based recommendations were common [@Lowry_2023].

**Role in care pathways.** Conservative management of partial-thickness rotator cuff tears and shoulder osteoarthritis consistently positions CSI as one component within a multimodal approach that includes physiotherapy, activity modification, and NSAIDs [@Longo_2023; @Yamamoto_2023; @Hsieh_2023].

### Injection Technique and Image Guidance

This theme (117 articles) covers the technical aspects of CSI delivery, including guidance modality, injection site, needle approach, and dosing.

**Ultrasound guidance versus landmark technique.** Ultrasound (US)-guided injections showed greater accuracy for most shoulder girdle injections compared with landmark-guided approaches. For the acromioclavicular joint, accuracy was 93.6% with US guidance versus 68.2% with landmarks (p<0.0001) [@Aly_2015]. For the biceps tendon sheath, accuracy was 86.7% with US versus 26.7% with landmarks [@Aly_2015]. However, for the subacromial space specifically, accuracy was similar between approaches (65% vs 70%) [@Aly_2015], likely because the subacromial bursa is a relatively large target.

**Injection site and approach.** CSI can be delivered into the subacromial bursa, the glenohumeral joint, or periarticular tissues, depending on the clinical indication [@Xiao_2017]. Dual-target injection (subacromial bursa plus proximal biceps long-head tendon) showed similar short-term efficacy to standard subacromial injection but with extended duration of benefit (n=60) [@Wang_2019]. Multisite injection showed lower early pain scores and better early function scores than single rotator interval injection for frozen shoulder [@Deng_2023].

**Dosing.** Ultrasound-guided hydrodilatation with 40 mg and 10 mg triamcinolone acetonide yielded similar improvements in SPADI, VAS, and ROM at 12-week follow-up (n=84), suggesting that lower doses may be preferable given potential detrimental effects of corticosteroids on adjacent cartilage and tendons [@Wang_2023]. Similarly, capsule-preserving hydrodilatation with 5% dextrose water yielded comparable improvements to glucocorticoid-based hydrodilatation for frozen shoulder (n=84) [@Yao_2025].

**Ultrasound-guided percutaneous irrigation (US-PICT).** For calcific tendinopathy, US-guided percutaneous irrigation is well-tolerated, with 70% of patients reporting no pain during the procedure [@Albano_2020]. Procedure duration differs between single-needle and double-needle techniques depending on calcification consistency, with the double-needle approach faster for hard calcifications and the single-needle approach faster for fluid calcifications [@Orlandi_2017].

**Predictive factors for injection response.** A prospective study of 100 patients found that the presence of subacromial bursitis on ultrasound did not influence clinical outcomes at 3 months post-CSI, suggesting that ultrasonographic bursitis should not guide injection indication [@Metayer_2024]. A posterior acromial slope greater than 36 degrees and the presence of calcific tendinitis were associated with better outcomes after subacromial injection, with 86.4% of patients with a slope greater than 36 degrees showing improvement [@Dietrich_2013].

### Safety, Adverse Effects, and Tissue Impact of Corticosteroids

This theme (101 articles) addresses the safety profile of CSI, including local and systemic adverse effects and the impact of corticosteroids on tendon tissue.

**General safety profile.** The GRASP trial reported no serious adverse events related to CSI in 708 randomised participants [@Hopewell_2021a; @Hopewell_2021b]. Adverse event rates in trials comparing CSI with manual therapy and exercise were similar across groups [@Page_2014]. Overall, the acute safety profile of single CSI is well-established, with most adverse effects being mild and self-limiting (injection site pain, transient flushing).

**Tendon-level effects.** Mechanistic concerns arise from studies examining corticosteroid effects on tendon biology. Glucocorticoid injection induced specific ion-channel-mediated toxicity in human rotator cuff tendon, with increases in the glutamate receptor NMDAR1 after CSI raising concerns about potential excitotoxic tendon damage [@Dean_2014]. In contrast, surgical rotator cuff repair produced increases in cell proliferation, vascularity, and HIF-1-alpha consistent with a healing response, which were not observed after CSI [@Dean_2014].

**Repeated injection concerns.** The cumulative effect of repeated CSI is an area of ongoing concern. The evidence suggests dose-dependent and time-dependent effects on tendon integrity, though direct human data on repeated subacromial injections remain limited. Multiple meta-analyses and systematic reviews note that corticosteroid injections for tendinopathy demonstrate short-term benefit followed by intermediate-term worsening compared with other interventions or placebo [@Coombes_2010].

**Perioperative CSI and surgical risk.** Preoperative CSI use is associated with potential increases in revision surgery risk. A meta-analysis found that patients with corticosteroid injections had a pooled increased risk of revision surgery by 47% (odds ratio 1.44, 95% CI 1.36-1.52) [@Darbandi_2024]. Periarticular corticosteroid injections combined with multimodal analgesia did not alter tendon healing, clinical outcomes, or functional outcomes 1 year after arthroscopic rotator cuff repair [@Perdreau_2020], with retear rates of 16% in the study group versus 36% in the control group (p=0.107) [@Perdreau_2020].

**Chronic steroid exposure.** In a large cohort (n=39,876), chronic steroid use was not an independent risk factor for infection, readmission, or reoperation following arthroscopic rotator cuff repair, but was independently associated with higher rates of 30-day mortality [@Richelle_2023].

**Alternative injectables and safety.** The safety advantage of non-steroidal alternatives is a recurring theme. NSAID injections offer a similar efficacy profile without the tissue-level concerns of corticosteroids [@Ziradkar_2023]. Radial extracorporeal shockwave therapy (rESWT) has been proposed as a safe alternative to CSI in diabetic patients with adhesive capsulitis, showing superior short-term function improvement [@El_2020].

### CSI for Adhesive Capsulitis and Frozen Shoulder

This theme (67 articles) specifically addresses CSI use in adhesive capsulitis (AC), a condition where intra-articular injection and hydrodilatation play a distinct role compared with subacromial injection for impingement.

**Intra-articular CSI efficacy.** Corticosteroid injections for adhesive capsulitis demonstrate short-term efficacy but may not provide long-term benefit [@Xiao_2017]. A meta-analysis of 5 RCTs (225 patients) found that intra-articular CSI were more effective than placebo in pain relief and ROM improvement at 0-8 weeks and 9-24 weeks, but the effect did not persist beyond this period [@Wang_2017].

**Hydrodilatation with corticosteroid.** Hydrodilatation with corticosteroids is the most effective conservative management for frozen shoulder based on an umbrella review of 8 meta-analyses [@Lädermann_2021]. Combining hydrodilatation with corticosteroid injection potentially expedites recovery of pain-free ROM, with the greatest benefit experienced within the first 3 months [@Catapano_2018]. A team approach combining suprascapular nerve block, glenohumeral joint hydrodilatation, and physiotherapy showed significant improvements in forward flexion (median 25 degrees) and external rotation (median 19 degrees), with no complications reported [@Dakkak_2024].

**Timing of injection.** Early injection appears to improve outcomes at both short- and long-term follow-ups in adhesive capsulitis. If pain persists despite non-invasive treatments, early injection may be considered to shorten the natural history of the condition (n=339) [@Ahn_2018].

**CSI versus suprascapular nerve block.** Both suprascapular nerve block (SSNB) and intra-articular corticosteroid injection are effective for adhesive capsulitis. SSNB may offer increased pain tolerability for effective mobilization with persistent effects at 12 weeks (n=86) [@Haque_2021].

**Corticosteroid dosing for frozen shoulder.** Ultrasound-guided hydrodilatation with 40 mg versus 10 mg triamcinolone acetonide yielded similar improvements, supporting lower-dose approaches given potential tissue-level concerns (n=84) [@Wang_2023]. The comparison between multisite injection and single rotator interval injection showed that multisite injection produced lower early pain scores (VAS 3.1 versus 4.3 at 4 weeks) and better early function (n=59) [@Deng_2023].

**Non-corticosteroid alternatives.** Radial extracorporeal shockwave therapy was superior to low-dose intra-articular CSI for function improvement in diabetic patients with AC at short-term follow-up (n=103) [@El_2020]. Dextrose water (D5W) hydrodilatation yielded comparable results to glucocorticoid-based hydrodilatation, potentially offering a corticosteroid-free alternative (n=84) [@Yao_2025].

**Oral versus injectable corticosteroids.** Oral corticosteroids and exercises both improved frozen shoulder outcomes, but the exercise group demonstrated superior functional improvement with larger effect sizes (n=33) [@Çelik_2023].

### CSI Impact on Rotator Cuff Repair and Surgical Outcomes

This theme (64 articles) examines the relationship between CSI use and subsequent surgical outcomes, particularly for rotator cuff repair.

**Preoperative CSI and revision risk.** A critical finding is that preoperative CSI within 6 months of rotator cuff repair (RCR) is associated with increased revision risk. An analysis of 4,959 arthroscopic RCR subjects found that patients who received injection within 6 months prior to surgery were significantly more likely to undergo revision cuff repair within 3 years, and the risk significantly declined when more than 6 months elapsed between injection and surgery [@Traven_2019]. A systematic review and meta-analysis of 10 studies (n=240,976) found that 20.0% received a perioperative CSI, and preoperative CSI was the most consistently identified risk factor for revision surgery [@Darbandi_2024; @Kunze_2020]. The incidence of revision RCR ranged from 3.8% to 10.5% with preoperative CSI versus 3.2% without [@Kunze_2020].

**Postoperative CSI.** Five studies examining postoperative CSI and retear rates showed incidences ranging from 5.7% to 19.0% in patients who received postoperative CSI [@Kunze_2020]. Postoperative subacromial corticosteroid injections are commonly used, with approximately one in five patients receiving them within 6 months of non-arthroplasty shoulder surgery, and over 95% reporting that injections reduced pain and improved function 6 weeks post-injection [@Skedros_2017].

**Periarticular CSI at time of surgery.** In contrast to the concerns about preoperative CSI, periarticular injection of corticosteroids combined with multimodal analgesia at the time of arthroscopic RCR did not alter tendon healing or clinical outcomes at 1-year follow-up (n=50, retear rates 16% vs 36%, p=0.107) [@Perdreau_2020].

**Value of surgery itself.** The CSAW trial (n=313) showed that arthroscopic subacromial decompression offered no extra benefit over arthroscopy alone (placebo surgery), questioning the value of this operation. Both surgical groups showed a small benefit over no treatment (mean Oxford Shoulder Score difference 2.8-4.2 points), but these differences were not clinically important [@Beard_2018]. A Cochrane review of 9 trials (1,007 participants) found uncertainty about whether rotator cuff repair surgery provides clinically meaningful benefits over non-operative management [@Karjalainen_2019].

**Risk factors for healing failure.** Beyond CSI, age and BMI are independent predictive factors for tendon healing after rotator cuff repair in small-to-medium-sized tears (n=94) [@A._2022b]. Bone microvascularization of the footprint plays a role in healing, with lower microvessel rates decreasing tendon integrity and healing potential [@Bonnevialle_2015].

**Expert consensus.** An expert panel achieved consensus that impingement is a clinical diagnosis, that physiotherapy should always be the first-line treatment, that CSI can be helpful in reducing symptoms, and that surgical indications should be reserved for treatment failures [@Hohmann_2022].

### Calcific Tendinopathy Management

This theme (41 articles) addresses the specific role of CSI and procedural interventions in calcific tendinopathy of the rotator cuff.

**Subacromial CSI for calcific tendinopathy.** Corticosteroid injection after ultrasound-guided needling and lavage (barbotage) improved pain in the 6 weeks following the procedure and function in the 3 months after, with no significant effect on calcification resorption [@Darrieutort-Laffite_2019]. However, non-inferiority of saline compared with steroids could not be established in a randomised double-blind trial (n=132) [@Darrieutort-Laffite_2019].

**Ultrasound-guided percutaneous lavage.** US-guided percutaneous lavage (UGPL) has demonstrated clinical efficacy for calcific tendinopathy. Compared with extracorporeal shockwave therapy (ESWT), UGPL may be superior in clinical pain relief and calcification clearance [@Zhang_2019]. After barbotage combined with subacromial CSI versus isolated subacromial CSI, no significant differences were found in clinical and radiological outcomes at long-term follow-up [@de_2017].

**Steroid type matters.** A comparison of triamcinolone acetonide (TA) versus methylprednisolone acetonide (MA) after US-guided percutaneous irrigation showed similar short-term improvement, but TA produced higher long-term improvement (Constant Score delta: 53 versus 44 points at 180 days) and a higher chance of functional recovery (n=40) [@Battaglia_2017].

**Shockwave therapy as comparator.** A Cochrane review of 32 trials (2,281 participants) found very few clinically important benefits of shockwave therapy for rotator cuff disease, with wide clinical diversity and varying treatment protocols limiting conclusions [@Surace_2020]. For shoulder impingement specifically, ESWT added to conventional physiotherapy produced more noticeable intermediate-term effects than physiotherapy plus local CSI [@ElGendy_2023].

**Predictors of treatment success after percutaneous irrigation.** Large calcifications and low-grade pain at baseline correlated with short- and long-term pain improvement after US-guided percutaneous irrigation (n=79) [@Vassalou_2021]. The degree of calcium removal did not impact pain or functional improvement beyond 1 week [@Vassalou_2021].

**Operative versus non-operative.** A retrospective comparison (n=180) found no significant overall difference between operative and non-operative treatment for calcific tendinopathy, but for patients without concurrent rotator cuff tears, operative treatment showed better outcomes [@Fanglin_2024].

**US-guided needling versus ESWT.** Both modalities improved clinical outcomes and eliminated calcium deposits, but US-guided needling was more effective for short-term function restoration and pain relief [@Kim_2014].

## Knowledge Gaps

**Unanswered research questions:**

- Optimal number, timing, and interval of repeated CSI before escalation to surgery
- Long-term tendon-level effects of repeated subacromial CSI in humans (most mechanistic data come from animal or in vitro studies)
- Comparative effectiveness of specific corticosteroid formulations (triamcinolone vs methylprednisolone vs betamethasone vs dexamethasone) for subacromial injection
- Patient-level predictive models for CSI response to enable personalized treatment selection

**Methodological gaps:**

- Lack of standardization in outcome measures, injection protocols, and follow-up durations across trials
- Most studies evaluate single injections; the cumulative effect of repeated injections is poorly characterized
- Limited cost-effectiveness analyses comparing CSI-first versus physiotherapy-first care pathways

**Underrepresented populations:**

- Diabetic patients (altered healing, specific steroid concerns) are studied primarily in the context of adhesive capsulitis
- Elderly populations (>75 years) are underrepresented despite being a high-prevalence group
- Workers' compensation populations, where psychosocial factors may modify treatment response
- French-language studies and French institutional data are sparse

**Translational gaps:**

- Translation of mechanistic tendon toxicity findings to clinical dosing recommendations
- Integration of imaging biomarkers (ultrasound morphology, MRI findings) into treatment algorithms
- Standardized care pathway protocols incorporating CSI timing relative to physiotherapy and surgical planning

# Discussion

## Main Findings

**Principal findings:**

1. **Short-term efficacy is established, long-term benefit is not.** CSI consistently provides significant pain relief and functional improvement for 2-8 weeks. Beyond 3-6 months, CSI offers no demonstrable advantage over placebo or physiotherapy alone, based on the largest available RCTs [@Hopewell_2021a; @Beard_2018].
2. **CSI is most effective as a bridge to rehabilitation.** Combined with physiotherapy, CSI serves as a facilitator enabling patients to engage with exercise programs. CSI alone is consistently inferior to combined approaches [@Lavoie-Gagne_2022; @Raeesi_2023].
3. **Preoperative CSI timing matters for surgical outcomes.** CSI within 6 months of rotator cuff repair is associated with increased revision risk, while more than 6 months between injection and surgery significantly reduces this risk [@Traven_2019; @Darbandi_2024].
4. **PRP emerges as a long-term alternative.** While CSI provides faster short-term relief, PRP shows superior long-term outcomes for rotator cuff tendinopathy, with lower failure rates at 12 months [@Rossi_2024; @Pang_2023].
5. **Lower corticosteroid doses may suffice.** Evidence from hydrodilatation trials suggests that reduced corticosteroid doses produce equivalent clinical outcomes, supporting dose minimization strategies given tissue-level safety concerns [@Wang_2023; @Yao_2025].

**Consensus areas:**

- Short-term CSI efficacy for pain relief
- Physiotherapy as first-line treatment, with CSI as adjunct
- US guidance improves accuracy for specific injection sites (AC joint, biceps sheath) but may not be necessary for subacromial space
- Hydrodilatation with corticosteroid is effective for adhesive capsulitis

**Controversies:**

- Optimal timing and number of CSI before surgery
- Whether CSI causes clinically meaningful tendon damage in vivo
- Role of CSI versus PRP as preferred injectable
- Value of subacromial decompression surgery

## Interpretation and Implications

**Context:** These findings align with the evolving understanding that subacromial pain is often a self-limiting condition where the natural history favours improvement. CSI accelerates the early phase of recovery but does not alter the disease trajectory. This interpretation is supported by the CSAW trial finding that even placebo surgery produced improvement comparable to actual decompression [@Beard_2018].

**Mechanisms:** The short-term benefit of CSI is likely mediated through local anti-inflammatory effects on the subacromial bursa and surrounding soft tissues. The lack of sustained benefit may reflect the time-limited nature of corticosteroid action combined with potential counterproductive effects on tendon biology, including inhibition of tenocyte proliferation and collagen synthesis [@Dean_2014].

**Implications for:**

- **Clinical practice:** CSI should be positioned as a short-term analgesic bridge to enable physiotherapy engagement, not as a standalone treatment. A maximum of 2-3 injections before reassessing the management plan is supported by the evidence. For patients approaching rotator cuff repair, a minimum 6-month interval between last CSI and surgery should be observed. In adhesive capsulitis, hydrodilatation with corticosteroid is the most effective conservative option and should be offered early.
- **Policy:** Care pathway protocols (such as PRISE) should formalize the CSI-physiotherapy sequence, specify maximum injection frequency, and mandate a washout period before surgical referral. Cost-effectiveness analyses of CSI-first versus physiotherapy-first pathways are needed to guide resource allocation.
- **Research:** Priority research directions include head-to-head comparison of corticosteroid formulations, dose-finding studies for subacromial injection, long-term prospective cohort studies tracking tendon integrity after repeated CSI, and pragmatic trials comparing CSI-based care pathways with PRP-based alternatives.

## Strengths and Limitations

**Strengths:** This scoping review encompasses a large and comprehensive evidence base (358 articles) spanning 16 years, identified through three databases. The breadth of the review captures the full landscape of CSI-related evidence, from efficacy trials to mechanistic studies, care pathway analyses, and safety data.

**Limitations:**

- **Search and selection:** AI-assisted single-reviewer screening may have introduced selection bias. Language was restricted to English and French, potentially missing relevant studies in other languages. Grey literature was not systematically searched.
- **Methodological:** As a scoping review, no formal quality assessment or meta-analytic synthesis was performed. The heterogeneity of study designs, populations, and outcome measures limits the strength of cross-study comparisons.
- **Temporal:** The rapid evolution of evidence, particularly regarding PRP and other biologics, means that the most recent trials may shift the balance of evidence.
- **Extraction:** Claims were extracted from abstracts only; full-text data extraction would provide more granular and nuanced findings.

**Impact:** These limitations primarily affect the precision of effect size estimates and the certainty of comparative conclusions. The overall direction and consistency of findings across themes are unlikely to be altered by these limitations.

## Future Research

**Priority questions:**

1. **Optimal CSI protocol before surgery** - What is the minimum safe interval between last CSI and rotator cuff repair? A prospective cohort study with standardized injection protocols and MRI-based tendon assessment would provide actionable data. Expected impact: direct clinical guideline change.
2. **CSI versus PRP cost-effectiveness** - Is PRP cost-effective compared with CSI when accounting for long-term outcomes and avoidance of surgery? A pragmatic RCT with economic evaluation would address this. Expected impact: formulary and reimbursement decisions.
3. **Repeated injection tendon effects** - Do repeated subacromial CSI cause cumulative tendon damage detectable on imaging? A prospective cohort with serial MRI would fill this critical gap. Expected impact: evidence-based injection frequency limits.

**Recommendations:** Future studies should adopt standardized outcome measures (SPADI, VAS, ASES), specify corticosteroid type and dose, report follow-up beyond 12 months, and include patient-reported outcomes beyond pain and function (return to work, quality of life, treatment satisfaction).

# Conclusions

1. **Efficacy**: Subacromial corticosteroid injections provide reliable short-term pain relief (2-8 weeks) for shoulder pain and rotator cuff disorders, but offer no demonstrated long-term benefit beyond 3-6 months compared with placebo or physiotherapy alone.
2. **Care pathway placement**: CSI is most effective when combined with physiotherapy as a first-line approach, serving as a bridge to enable rehabilitation engagement. CSI alone is consistently inferior to combined approaches.
3. **Surgical considerations**: A minimum 6-month interval between last CSI and planned rotator cuff repair is advisable, given evidence of increased revision risk with more proximate injection timing.
4. **Safety**: The acute safety profile of CSI is favourable, but mechanistic evidence raises concerns about cumulative tendon effects that remain inadequately characterized in clinical studies. Lower corticosteroid doses appear to produce equivalent outcomes and may reduce tissue-level risks.
5. **Emerging alternatives**: PRP shows promising long-term superiority over CSI for rotator cuff tendinopathy and deserves consideration as an alternative injectable in appropriate clinical contexts.

**Evidence certainty:** Per PRISMA-ScR, formal certainty assessment is not required. The evidence base is large but heterogeneous, with consistent directional findings across themes supporting the conclusions above.
**Translation readiness:** The short-term efficacy of CSI and the combined CSI-physiotherapy approach are ready for clinical implementation. Optimal injection protocols and the role of PRP require additional evidence before standardized recommendation.
